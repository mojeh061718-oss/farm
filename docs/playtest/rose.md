# Harvest Empire playtest — Rose, 68 ("Rosie's Patch", Cozy, 100 days)

Persona: retired teacher, first video game beyond solitaire. Followed the goal chip exclusively, kept a 12-16 tile plot, watered diligently but forgot the game entirely about one day in five, hoarded her harvest, and never read a manual because there isn't one.

Final state (day 100): Level 9, $1,190 in coins, farm value $21,366, 8 animals, 7 buildings, 3 land parcels, 540 crops harvested, 88 crops lost, 17 loaves baked, current goal "Own 4 land parcels."

---

## 1. Weekly diary

**Week 1 (spring, year 1).** Emma set this up on my phone and called it Rosie's Patch. The little card at the top told me to till four plots, then plant, then water, then harvest, and each time I did it a small purse of money appeared — like gold stars, and I have always liked gold stars. By Thursday I had built a $500 coop because the card said to, and met my first hen, Pumpernickel. A storm took one turnip and a message said something about scarecrows, but the card hadn't mentioned scarecrows, so neither did I. I missed Friday — the church bazaar takes it out of you.

**Week 2 (summer, year 1).** A terrible week and I shall be honest about it. Summer "arrived" — whatever that means in a place with no calendar on the wall — and overnight all twelve of my turnips went grey and crumbly. I replanted the whole bed. Dead again by morning, twenty-four little corpses in two days. There was a red message about "out of season," but the shop sold me those seeds without a word of warning. I stopped buying seed packets altogether and just fed Pumpernickel. What else was there to do?

**Week 3 (fall into winter, year 1).** Empty beds, one hen, and a card that said "Collect 5 animal products" for ten straight days. Pumpernickel lays at her own pace, thank you very much. We got there Thursday and the card moved on to "Complete 2 orders" — those pinned notes on the board I'd been ignoring since day one. Winter came. I had nothing planted, so for once nothing died.

**Week 4 (winter into spring, year 2).** Emma visited Sunday and explained two things nobody at the farm ever did. First, the seed packets have tiny season badges — you cannot grow a spring turnip in July, apparently. Second, you can politely *decline* an order; the board had been clogged for a week with requests for things I have never grown and could not spell. Spring came back and we planted carrots together. It felt like being allowed to start over.

**Week 5 (spring into summer, year 2).** I treated myself. The shop had a shiny red "Rototiller," $450 — nearly a tenth of everything I had. The receipt said it "burns 0.05 gal per tile." Gallons of WHAT? I took it to the side strip and a message scolded me: "Out of fuel — hand-tilling one plot. Buy fuel in the Shop!" Nobody said one word about fuel when they took my $450. I dug the row by hand, same as always. A storm smashed three carrots midweek. Still no scarecrow — the card hasn't asked.

**Week 6 (summer into fall, year 2).** A week of that machine sulking by the fence. I finally telephoned Emma, who explained there is a fuel pump hiding in the shop. Two gallons of diesel: six dollars. Six! For a push-tiller I paid $450 for. With the diesel in it chewed up the last of the side strip in one go and used barely a thimbleful. The rest of my garden was already dug by hand, so the wonder machine is now ornamental. Sixteen beds under carrot now, though.

**Week 7 (winter, year 2).** Quiet and white. I grow garlic in winter now — Emma's idea — and it doesn't seem to mind the frost. On the last day of the season somebody in town finally wanted what I had: my first order filled, $305, twenty-nine days after the card first asked me to do two of them. One down. The cupboard, meanwhile, is getting embarrassing: hundreds of vegetables and eggs I cannot bring myself to sell.

**Week 8 (spring, year 3).** A gentle week, and gentle weeks make me suspicious now. Carrots in, carrots out, eggs from Pumpernickel, garlic sold to no one. The card still says "Complete 2 orders." The board still asks for tomatoes, melons, things I have never once planted. I decline the stalest note every so often, like returning a library book unread.

**Week 9 (summer, year 3).** The worst week since the turnips. Heatwave after heatwave — the game says crops dry out three times faster — and on the very day I clean forgot the farm (bridge club, then Margaret needed a ride), all sixteen plants died of thirst. Sixteen. One missed day, in the wrong week, and the whole garden gone. On Cozy, mind you. I replanted with my jaw set. Also summer's arrival had already withered my sixteen spring carrots the week's first morning — the calendar kills more than the weather here.

**Week 10 (fall, year 3).** Rebuilt again. Here is the strange arithmetic of my life now: my cupboard holds over 340 items the game values near $10,000, and my purse holds about $900, and I feel poor. Nothing in the game suggests selling; nothing warns against hoarding; the card only says, still, "Complete 2 orders."

**Week 11 (winter y3 into spring y4).** The dam broke. Tuesday my purse hit $412, the lowest it has ever been. Wednesday an order finally matched my cupboard — $450, goal complete, FIFTY-FOUR DAYS after the card first asked. And then everything happened at once: the card demanded a scarecrow (built it — $350 for a pumpkin on a stick, and no one will tell me how far its protection reaches), then a second field ($1,000). To afford the field I finally emptied the cupboard: $10,605 in one glorious market day. Twelve thousand dollars of vegetables had been sitting in there while I pinched pennies. I laughed and then I sat down.

**Week 12 (spring into summer, year 4).** Rich, briefly. Fertilized five crops because the card said to — $20 a sprinkle and I could not tell you what the powder does; nothing looked different. Then a $3,200 bakery for "artisan goods." Summer arrived and withered eleven more carrots (I never learn the calendar's tricks in time), but Friday my first loaf came out of the oven. Sixty years of baking bread, and this is the first loaf that ever earned me a goal star. Bread wants my wheat and my eggs. At last, a task that makes sense to a grandmother.

**Week 13 (fall, year 4).** The card wants EIGHT animals. Eight! I bought hens like a woman possessed — Nugget, Mochi, Olive — $150 apiece. A crow ate a carrot right off the vine midweek; apparently my pumpkin-on-a-stick does not cover that corner, though no one will show me its borders. Feeding this crowd costs real money every single morning now. Nobody mentioned animals would be a daily bill.

**Week 14 (winter y4 into spring y5).** What a week. Daisy and Pickles joined the coop; the game refused me a seventh hen — the coop is "full at six," which it never mentioned until it refused. So: a $1,500 barn, then Maple the cow, then Waffles the cow ($850 each — she is enormous and I love her). Saturday the register rang all day: a $1,060 order, the eight-animals star, the $10,000-lifetime star. Emma counted the cupboard, laughed until she cried, and now the morning harvest goes straight to market. Then the card asked for a sprinkler (bought; it waters the same two or three plants and ignores the rest) and a HARVEST DRONE. Seven thousand five hundred dollars. For a flying robot. On a farm. I bought it. Emma will not believe it.

**Week 15 (spring, year 5 — days 99-100).** The robot flew exactly one morning — harvested three carrots, replanted three — and then sat dark on its post: "grounded, no fuel." Surely a $7,500 machine comes with a full tank? It drinks a gallon of diesel a day, which is more than my tiller ever did, and I found this out the same way I find out everything here: after paying. The card now says "Own 4 land parcels" (I bought a third field Friday — more mowing I cannot keep up with). Day one hundred I forgot the farm entirely, bridge club again, and the robot presumably sat hungry. One hundred days, eight animals, a bakery, an ornamental tiller, and a granddaughter who is very proud of me. I shall keep going. But someone should sit this game down and make it explain itself.

## 2. What I LOVED

1. **The gold-star machine.** The first six goals (till, plant, water, harvest, sell, coop) are perfect onboarding: one verb at a time, an immediate cash reward, no reading required. Rose completed five goals in four days and felt like a natural.
2. **The animals have names before you meet them.** Pumpernickel, Biscuit, Waffles, Maple. Instant, effortless attachment — Rose refers to hens by name in every note. Cheap feature, enormous warmth.
3. **Cozy actually protected her from ruin.** $6,000 starting nest egg meant 88 dead crops, a $450 impulse purchase, and chronic hoarding never once ended the run. Minimum purse across 100 days: $412. She never went broke, never lost an animal, was never punished into quitting.
4. **Rain days announce themselves kindly.** "Rain today — no watering needed!" (20 times in her run) is exactly the tone a nervous newcomer needs: the game telling you it's okay to relax.
5. **Batched bad news.** "Crop losses: 16 died of thirst" as one message instead of sixteen separate alarms. The worst morning of her run was one sentence, not a barrage.
6. **Bread makes sense.** Wheat plus egg equals bread is the one production chain a 68-year-old understands instantly, and it landed emotionally ("the first loaf that ever earned me a goal star").
7. **Safety nets exist everywhere** (odd jobs, 50% building sell-back, order decline, out-of-season warning toast). Rose found almost none of them unaided — but they were there when Emma pointed.

## 3. What confused or upset me (the important part)

1. **Seasons are the deadliest unexplained mechanic in the game.** 67 of her 88 lost crops (76%) died "out of season" — and only 24 of those were her turnip mistake. The other 43 were *legally planted* crops (spring carrots, fall carrots) killed silently the morning a new season arrived. The shop happily sells out-of-season seeds; the only warning is a toast *after* your money is spent ("the seed money is gone"); and standing crops get no warning at all before the calendar executes them. Rose stopped planting entirely for 16 days in year 1 because she concluded she was cursed.
2. **Fuel is a hidden second price tag — twice.** The rototiller ($450) arrives with an empty tank and its fuel need is disclosed in one post-purchase toast. Rose spent 7 days being scolded ("Out of fuel — hand-tilling one plot") before buying $6 of diesel. Then the game did it again at 16x the price: the $7,500 drone flew one morning, ran the tank below 1 gallon, and was grounded for the rest of the run. Nothing at either purchase says "this machine needs diesel, tank currently: 0."
3. **The goal chip abandoned her for 54 days.** "Complete 2 orders" was her active goal from day 18 to day 72 — more than half the playthrough. The linear chain means one stuck goal freezes ALL guidance; her notes say it plainly: "I do not rightly know how to hurry it along, and the game is not saying." Everything downstream (scarecrow! second parcel! fertilizer!) was held hostage.
4. **The order board is a riddle with no rules.** Two orders are pinned from minute one with zero explanation. Orders request items she has never grown; unfulfillable orders never expire; new orders only appear when a slot opens; and the decline button is undiscoverable. Her board was effectively deadlocked until "Emma" explained skipping.
5. **The scarecrow arrives 60+ days after the crows do.** The game's own disaster messages advertise scarecrows from week 1 (storm, day 3), but the goal chip — a rule-follower's only map — doesn't ask for one until goal 9 (day 73 for Rose, thanks to the order deadlock). She lost crops to two storms and a crow while the solution sat unmentioned in her bible. And once placed, its 5x5 coverage is invisible — a crow later ate a carrot from an uncovered corner and she had no way to see why.
6. **Nothing ever says "sell your things."** A hoarder by nature, Rose reached day 76 with 381 items worth $12,083 in inventory and $853 in coins, feeling poor the entire time. The economy assumes you know goods are for selling; there is no cupboard-value display, no nudge, nothing.
7. **Invisible mechanics she paid for and never understood:** fertilizer ($20/sprinkle, zero visible effect — it's actually +25% growth and better double-harvest odds, said nowhere), coop capacity (discovered only when the game refused hen #7), daily feed bills (about $54/day by week 14, never disclosed; the Feed Mill that would fix it is never once suggested), and the sprinkler's tiny 3x3 area ("it waters the same two or three plants and ignores the rest").
8. **"Collect 5 animal products" with one hen is a two-week wait.** The goal arrived on day 4 and completed on day 18. The card never hints that buying a second hen is allowed, so a literal-minded player just... waits. Ten of her hundred days were spent staring at that one goal with genuinely nothing to do.

## 4. Bugs & oddities (as a newcomer experiences them)

1. **Powered equipment sells with an empty tank and no affordance.** Both fuel-burning purchases (tiller, drone) start unusable. Evidence: "Upgraded to the Rototiller!" followed the same morning by "Out of fuel — hand-tilling one plot" (y2 summer d1); "Harvest Drone built!" (day 98) followed by "A drone is grounded — no fuel!" from day 100 after a single flight. Whether bug or design, to a newcomer it reads as *broken merchandise*.
2. **The rototiller is nearly worthless by the time anyone buys it.** Tilling is one-time — soil never reverts to grass — so a small-plot player has almost nothing left to till. Rose's lifetime tilled count: 12 tiles, 8 of them by hand on day 1. Her $450 machine tilled 4 tiles ever, then decorated a fence. An upgrade whose utility silently expires feels like a trap.
3. **Order-board deadlock is a systemic stall.** With 4 unfulfillable orders pinned, no new orders generate (refill only triggers when count < 4) and none expire. Combined with the linear goal chain this froze goal progression for 54 in-game days. Reproducible for any player who doesn't find the decline button.
4. **Season change kills without a warning event.** There is a weather forecast, but no "season ends tomorrow" signal, and healthy in-season crops become dead "out of season" ones overnight. Evidence: "Crop losses: 16 withered out of season" on the first morning of summer y3 — all were valid spring plantings.
5. **The out-of-season warning fires after payment.** Planting deducts seed cost, then toasts "it will wither and the seed money is gone!" — the message itself admits the sequence. Rose lost roughly $200 in seed money to withering before she understood.
6. **Toast spam vs. toast scarcity.** 288 toasts in 100 days, of which 103 were "Watering can refilled!" and 76 were "Fed N animals!" — while genuinely vital information (fuel requirements, coop capacity, scarecrow radius, fertilizer effect) never appears at all. The signal-to-noise ratio is inverted, and toasts vanish; there is no message log to re-read one you missed.
7. **Odd pacing artifact:** the "Fertilize 5 crops" goal (reward $150) costs $100 and 30 seconds; the goal before it ("Complete 2 orders," $180) can take 50+ days. Adjacent goals differ in effort by two orders of magnitude.

## 5. Suggestions for 2.0 (ranked, onboarding & accessibility first)

1. **Teach the calendar before it kills.** A one-line season primer on day 1; a "Last day of Spring!" banner; a warning icon on any planted crop that cannot ripen before the season flips; and a confirm dialog ("Turnips don't grow in Summer — plant anyway?") when buying/planting out-of-season seed. This single change would have prevented 76% of Rose's losses.
2. **Fuel onboarding.** Sell every powered machine with a starter tank (even 1 gallon), show "Requires diesel — tank: 0.0 gal" on the purchase screen, and pop a one-tap "Fill tank ($7)?" offer at purchase. The tiller and drone are the two worst moments in the whole run and both are the same missing sentence.
3. **Unfreeze the goal chip.** Offer 2-3 concurrent goals, or let a stuck goal be set aside ("Come back to this later") so the player's only guidance never deadlocks. Also reorder: scarecrow before the multi-week order goal, since the game's own disasters advertise it from week 1.
4. **Make orders self-healing and self-explaining.** First-time tooltip on the board; orders you cannot currently produce tagged ("You aren't growing tomatoes yet"); stale orders expire and refresh automatically after a few days; the decline button made prominent.
5. **Show coverage areas.** When placing (and when tapping) a scarecrow, sprinkler, or drone, highlight the protected/watered/harvested tiles. Rose paid for all three without ever once seeing what they touched.
6. **A "cupboard nudge" for hoarders.** Display total inventory value next to coins, and when inventory value exceeds ~5x coins, have the goal chip or a villager gently suggest market day. Rose sat on $12,083 while her purse held $412.
7. **Gentle failure forensics and undo.** Tapping a dead crop should say why it died (thirst / rot / out of season / frost / storm). Add a short buy-back window on big purchases — Rose would have returned the tiller — and a confirm on any purchase over ~10% of current coins.
8. **A readable journal.** Toasts vanish in seconds; 68-year-old eyes need a scrollable message log, larger-text mode, longer toast duration, and high-contrast wilt/dry indicators (a thin water bar is not enough).
9. **Right-size the early animal goal.** "Collect 5 products" should either scale to flock size, or the chip should hint "hens lay faster when happy — and you can house up to 6." Rose waited 14 days in silence.
10. **Disclose recurring costs at point of sale.** Coop capacity, feed cost/day, vet risk, drone fuel/day — a small "upkeep" line on every purchase card. Also surface the Feed Mill when weekly feed spend gets large; Rose paid cash feed for 100 days without ever learning grain feeding exists.
11. **Quality-of-life for small hands and short sessions:** a "morning chores" one-tap assistant (water + feed + collect, no strategic choices), since the daily loop included 103 can-refills of pure repetition on the starter can.
12. **Celebrate recoveries, not just streaks.** After a mass-death morning, a supportive message ("Every farmer loses a bed sometimes — replant at half seed price today") would turn the scariest moments into teaching moments instead of quit moments.

## 6. Balance notes — was Cozy actually cozy?

**Verdict: financially cozy, emotionally not.** The Cozy modifiers (0.5x events, $6,000 start) protect against the *random* hazards but not the *knowledge* hazards, and a newcomer's losses are almost entirely knowledge hazards.

Losses by cause (88 crops lost, from game toasts):

| Hazard | Crops lost | Cozy-mitigated? |
|---|---|---|
| Out-of-season wither (calendar) | 67 (76%) | No — event multiplier does not apply |
| Thirst (one forgotten day in a heatwave streak) | 16 (18%) | No — wilt rate is difficulty-independent |
| Storms (2 events, pre-scarecrow) | 4 | Yes (0.5x) |
| Crows (1 event) | 1 | Yes (0.5x) |
| Frost | 0 | Yes — and she only grew winter-safe garlic |
| Rot | 0 | She harvested daily |
| Animal sickness | 0 sick, $0 vet | Forgetting 1 day in 5 never dropped happiness to zero — genuinely forgiving |

The two systems Cozy softens (crows/storms/frost) cost her 5 crops; the two it does not touch (seasons, thirst) cost her 83. If Cozy is meant to be the "relax and learn" mode, the season-wither and wilt-death rates are the numbers to tune — or better, warn about.

**Did the goal chip keep her afloat?** No — the nest egg and the hoard did. Goal rewards paid $3,010 total across 15 completed goals, while the purchases those same goals demanded cost $19,850 (coop+hen $650, scarecrow $350, parcels $3,500, fertilizer $100, bakery $3,200, 5 hens $750, barn $1,500, 2 cows $1,700, sprinkler $600, drone $7,500). The chip pays back roughly 15 cents per dollar it commands. Orders added $2,625 (8 fulfilled). The remaining ~$15,900 of her $21,528 lifetime earnings came from raw crop/product sales — mostly one cathartic $10,605 hoard liquidation on day 77. On Classic ($3,000 start), Rose's playstyle would have hit a hard wall at the bakery, possibly at the coop.

**Pacing:** 15 goals in 100 days, but 54 of those days were spent stuck on one goal (orders) and 14 on another (collect 5 with one hen). The final 25 days completed 7 goals in a cash-rich sprint. The difficulty curve is not a curve; it is two long flat plateaus and a cliff of shopping.

**Fuel economics:** total diesel purchased in 100 days: 2 gallons, $6 — against $7,950 of fuel-burning equipment. The drone (1 gal/day, roughly $3.40/day) costs more per day in fuel than a single chicken earns; nothing communicates this before purchase.
